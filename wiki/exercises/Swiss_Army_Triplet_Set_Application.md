# Swiss Army Triplet: Drum Set Application
Applying the [[Swiss Army Triplet]] to the drum set involves breaking the rudiment down into its component parts to create melodic and textured [[Linear_Drumming|linear]] or layered patterns.

### Sticking Analysis
The standard 16th-note variation of the left-hand Swiss Army Triplet (L LR R) can be visualized as a repeated grouping of two 16th notes and one 16th rest for each hand.
- **Right Hand**: R _ R L ...
- **Left Hand**: L L _ L ...
- **Overlap**: The hands overlap specifically to facilitate the [[Flams]].

### Orchestration Strategies
1. **Splitting the Flam**: Move the primary hand (usually the right) to [[Tom-Toms]] or [[Cymbals]] while keeping the grace-note hand on the [[Snare_Drum]].
2. **Flam Width**: Experiment with the "space" between the grace note and primary note.
    - **Wide Flams**: Traditional rudimental spacing.
    - **Flat Flams**: Played as [[Double Stops]] with no audible space between notes.
3. **Three Limb Layering**: Place the right hand on the [[Hi-Hat]] and the left on the snare. By adding a [[Bass_Drum]] pattern on the "& ah" of the beat, you create a dense, "Tony Williams-esque" texture.

### Pro-Tips
- **Melodic Movement**: When moving the right hand around the kit, ensure the [[Left Hand]] maintains a consistent [[Ghost_Notes]] volume on the snare to provide a "bed" for the melody.
- **Integration**: Once comfortable, blend these groupings with standard 16th-note fills to create sophisticated, modern phrasing.
