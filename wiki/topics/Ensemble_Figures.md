# Ensemble Figures (合奏樂句)

Ensemble Figures (often called "hits") are specific rhythms that are played by the entire ensemble (the whole band) in unison. For a drummer, accurately interpreting and supporting these figures is a signature of professional musicianship.

## Notation and Placement

- **Staff Position**: In a drum chart, ensemble figures are written in the middle of [[The_Staff|the staff]].
- **Precedence**: They take precedence over **[[Time_Slashes|Time Slashes]]**. When a figure appears, the drummer must momentarily depart from the standard groove to accentuate the rhythm.
- **Long vs. Short**: The visual [[Duration|duration]] of the figure indicates how the drummer should orchestrate it:
    - **Long Notes** (Half notes, Dotted halves): Typically played with a **Crash Cymbal** and supported by the **[[Bass_Drum|Bass Drum]]**.
- **Short Notes** (Eighth or [[Sixteenth_Notes|sixteenth notes]]): Often played as a crisp **Snare [[Rimshot|Rimshot]]** or a dry hit on a muted surface.
- **Combined Hits**: Certain figures may combine the **[[Snare_Drum|Snare Drum]]**, **[[Bass_Drum|Bass Drum]]**, and **Floor Tom** with a cymbal crash for a thicker, more authoritative sound.

## Orchestration Strategies

- **Supporting the Figure**: The [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] almost always plays in unison with the ensemble figure to provide a "foundation."
- **Orchestrating Around the Kit**: For extended figures or those occurring in transition sections, the drummer may move the [[Accents|accents]] across the toms and cymbals while keeping the [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] constant.
- **Set-Up Fills**: One of the most important roles of a drummer is to play a short "set-up" fill (often just one or two beats) that leads the band into the ensemble figure, ensuring everyone hits together.
- **Interpretation Techniques**:
    - **Hand-to-Hand 16ths**: Playing continuous [[Sixteenth_Notes|sixteenth notes]] while accenting only the notes that match the ensemble figure.
    - **Right-Hand Lead**: The right hand plays the actual hits while the left hand fills in the "missing" rhythmic spaces at a lower dynamic ([[Ghost_Notes|ghost notes]]).

## Professional Significance

- **Marketability**: The ability to read complex ensemble figures "cold" (without practice) is essential for professional studio and big-band work.
- **Marking the Form**: Ensemble figures often occur at the end of a musical phrase (e.g., bar 8 or 12), helping to "summarize" a section before moving to the next.
- **Dynamic Control**: Figures allow for dramatic shifts, such as a **Stop** where the band hits together and immediately enters total silence (as cued by a "Fine" or "Coda").
