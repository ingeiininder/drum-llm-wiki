# Syncopated Rhythms
**Syncopation** is a rhythmic device that involves placing accents or notes on normally weak beats or [[Upbeat]] (e.g., the "&s" and "ahs" between the main beats), rather than on the strong [[Downbeat]]. This creates rhythmic tension, interest, and a sense of "off-beat" feel.

**Key Characteristics:**
*   **Emphasis on Weak Beats**: Notes or accents fall on beats that are typically unaccented.
*   **Anticipation or Delay**: Rhythms can anticipate a strong beat or delay a note that would normally fall on a strong beat.
*   **Rhythmic Drive**: Syncopation often provides a driving, forward-moving feel to music.

**Example:**
Instead of a [[Quarter Note]] falling squarely on beat 1, a syncopated rhythm might place an [[Eighth Note]] on beat 1, followed by a quarter note on the "&" of 1, which then sustains over beat 2. This shifts the emphasis away from the expected downbeat.

**Pro-Tip for Drummers:** Developing a strong internal [[Metronome]] and a solid understanding of [[Subdivision]] is essential for playing syncopated rhythms accurately. Practice counting out loud, emphasizing the upbeats where the syncopated notes fall. This helps to internalize the "push and pull" of syncopation and execute it with precision and groove. [[Ties]] are frequently used to notate syncopated rhythms clearly.
