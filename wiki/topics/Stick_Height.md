# Stick Height
[[Stick Height]] refers to the vertical distance a [[Drumstick]] travels before striking a drum and its resting position after a stroke. It is a crucial element in controlling dynamics, articulation, and speed in drumming.

**Relationship to Dynamics and Speed:**
*   **Higher Stick Height:** Generally results in a louder, more powerful stroke. It allows for greater momentum and is often used for accents or slower, more deliberate rhythms (e.g., [[Eighth Notes]] played with a [[Full Stroke Position]]).
*   **Lower Stick Height:** Generally results in a softer, more controlled stroke. It allows for faster repetition and is used for quieter passages or quicker rhythms (e.g., [[Sixteenth Notes]] played with a [[Half Stroke Position]]).

**Key Concepts:**
1.  **Full Stroke Position:** The stick starts from a high position (e.g., 8-12 inches above the drum) and returns to that same high position after striking, typically associated with the [[Free Stroke]] for louder, slower notes.
2.  **Half Stroke Position:** The stick starts from a mid-range position (e.g., 4-6 inches above the drum) and returns to that same mid-range position, used for medium dynamics and faster rhythms.
3.  **Low Resting Position:** After a [[Control Stroke]] or [[Down Stroke]], the stick is stopped at a low position (e.g., 1-2 inches above the drumhead). This prepares the hand for subsequent soft notes (taps) or an [[Up Stroke]] to prepare for an accent.

**Practice Considerations:**
*   **Consistency:** Practice maintaining consistent stick heights for specific dynamics and rhythms.
*   **Transitions:** Develop the ability to smoothly transition between different stick heights, for example, from a [[Free Stroke]] (high rebound) to a [[Down Stroke]] (low resting position).
*   **Monitoring:** Use a mirror or video to monitor your stick heights for accuracy and consistency.

**Pro-Tip:** Consciously controlling your [[Stick Height]] is one of the most effective ways to achieve dynamic contrast and precision. Many advanced techniques, such as the [[Moeller Technique]], rely heavily on manipulating stick height through various stroke types (down, tap, up, full) to create seamless dynamic shifts.
