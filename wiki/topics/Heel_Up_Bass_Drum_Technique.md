# Heel Up [[Bass_Drum_Technique|Bass Drum Technique]]
The Heel Up [[Bass_Drum_Technique|Bass Drum Technique]] is a widely used foot technique where the heel is slightly raised off the pedal board, typically about one to two inches. This position offers greater versatility in terms of [[Dynamics|dynamics]] and speed compared to [[Heel_Down_Bass_Drum_Technique]].

## Execution
1.  **Starting Position**: The heel is raised slightly off the pedal board (1-2 inches), with the ball of the foot resting on the pedal.
2.  **Pivot Point**: While the heel is up, the focus is still on the pivoting of the ankle to provide the primary stroke, similar to the heel-down position.
3.  **Muscle Engagement**: Raising the heel allows other muscles in the leg (beyond just the [[Shin_Muscle]]) to share the responsibility, making the technique less fatiguing. It allows for the engagement of some upper leg muscles for energy and power, though the primary stroke still originates from the ankle.

## Advantages
*   **Increased [[Dynamics|Dynamics]] and Speed**: Heel up provides more options for generating power and playing faster passages.
*   **Reduced Fatigue**: By distributing the workload across more leg muscles, it is less fatiguing on the shin muscle, allowing for longer periods of playing.
*   **Versatility**: It's highly adaptable for various musical styles and dynamic requirements.

## "Heel-Down Motion with a Heel-Up Position"
A helpful way to conceptualize heel-up is to think of it as a "heel-down motion with a heel-up position." The pivoting action of the ankle should be identical in both positions, ensuring that the primary control still comes from the ankle.

## Common Pitfalls & Pro-Tips
*   **"Knee Bobbing"**: A common mistake is using too much upper leg, causing the knee to "bob" up and down excessively like a piston. While the upper leg can contribute to power for [[Accents|accents]] or loud sections, generally the knee should only move sympathetically within the stroke. Focus on the ankle as the primary motion generator.
*   **Relaxation**: Maintain a relaxed foot and ankle to allow for fluid motion and efficient rebound.
*   **Full Range of Motion**: For louder playing, allow the [[Bass_Drum_Beater]] to come back as far as possible after each stroke. This utilizes the full range of motion the pedal naturally provides, conserving energy by letting the pedal do some of the work.
*   **Dynamic Control**: Practice creating a significant dynamic separation between unaccented notes (beater 1-3 inches from head) and accented notes (beater as far back as possible).
