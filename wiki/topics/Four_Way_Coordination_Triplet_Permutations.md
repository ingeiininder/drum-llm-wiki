# Four-Way Coordination: Triplet Bass Drum Permutations

This study focuses on developing independence between an alternating snare drum pattern and various bass drum permutations within an [[Eighth_Note_Triplet]] feel.

### Technical Framework
- **Hands**: Constant alternating sticking (RLRLRL) playing eighth-note triplets.
- **Accents**: Specific snare notes are accented to create rhythmic interest.
- **Feet**: The hi-hat plays a steady [[Ostinato]] (typically on beats 2 and 4, or all four quarter notes), while the bass drum performs "double" triplet positions.

### Permutation Logic
The exercises (Ex 8.16 - 8.19) move the bass drum through different "slots" of the triplet:
- **Single Positions**: Bass drum on the "1", the "trip", or the "let".
- **Double Positions**: Bass drum playing two out of the three triplet partials (e.g., the "1" and the "trip").

### Practice Objectives
1. **Dynamic Separation**: Create a "large and distinct" dynamic gap between accented snare notes and unaccented "ghost" notes.
2. **Three-Part Counting**: Count "1-trip-let, 2-trip-let" out loud while playing. This is critical for aligning the bass drum with the hand pattern.
3. **Ostinato Integration**: Once the snare and bass drum are synchronized, add the [[Hi-Hat Ostinato]] B to achieve true [[Four-Way Coordination]].

### Common Pitfalls
- **Flamming**: The bass drum and snare/hi-hat must land exactly together. If they are slightly offset, it creates a "flam" effect that ruins the groove's pocket.
- **Rushing Triplets**: Ensure the triplets remain wide and even; do not let them collapse into a "dotted-eighth sixteenth" feel.
