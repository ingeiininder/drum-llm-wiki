# Blues

The Blues is the foundation of virtually every major American musical form born in the 20th century, including Jazz, R&B, [[Rock_'n'_Roll|Rock 'n' Roll]], and Hip-Hop. Evolving from African-American work songs, spirituals, and field hollers, it is a "roots" music form centered around expression and specific structural patterns.

## The 12-Bar Blues Structure

The most common form in Blues is the **12-Bar Blues**, which uses a standardized harmonic progression over 12 measures:

- **Chord Structure (I-IV-V)**:
    - **Bars 1-4**: Dominated by the I chord (the tonic).
    - **Bars 5-8**: Typically moves to the IV chord (the subdominant) and back to I.
    - **Bars 9-12**: Features the V chord (the dominant), often leading back to I in a "turnaround."
- **Open Form**: The 12-bar blues is an "open form," meaning it can be repeated indefinitely in 12-bar increments during a performance.

## Key Terminology

- **The Head**: In Blues and Jazz, the "Head" refers to the thematic melody of the song. It is conventionally played at the beginning and the end of a performance.
- **Chorus**: Slang for "one time through the form." For example, a "two-chorus solo" lasts for 24 bars.
- **The Cue**: Musicians often signal the "top" of the form (the return to the Head) by patting their hand on top of their head.

## Drumming Style and Feel

- **[[Shuffle_Feel|Shuffle Feel]]**: The most standard Blues rhythm is the **Shuffle**, a triplet-based [[Groove|groove]] where the middle triplet is omitted ("broken triplet").
- **Marking Form**: In a standard Blues performance, drummers often signal section changes by changing their "riding surface"—playing the [[Hi-Hat|hi-hat]] during the head and switching to the [[Ride_Cymbal|ride cymbal]] during solo choruses.
- **Slow Blues**: Historically significant masters like **Stevie Ray Vaughan** are cited as reference points for the intensive feel required in slow 12-bar blues.
