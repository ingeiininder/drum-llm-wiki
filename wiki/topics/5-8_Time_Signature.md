# 5/8 [[Time_Signature|Time Signature]]

The **5/8 [[Time_Signature|time signature]]** consists of five [[Eighth_Notes|eighth notes]] per [[Measure|measure]]. It is a primary example of an asymmetrical [[Meter|meter]] used extensively in fusion, progressive [[Rock|rock]], and various world music traditions.

### Construction and Groupings
In 5/8, the eighth note receives the [[Beat|beat]]. The [[Measure|measure]] is almost always subdivided into two dominant pulses of unequal length:
1.  **3 + 2 Grouping**: The first [[Pulse|pulse]] is a [[Dotted_Quarter_Note|dotted quarter note]] (3 eighths), followed by a quarter note (2 eighths).
2.  **2 + 3 Grouping**: The first [[Pulse|pulse]] is a quarter note (2 eighths), followed by a [[Dotted_Quarter_Note|dotted quarter note]] (3 eighths).

### Counting Methods
When first learning 5/8, it is recommended to count every eighth note: **1 - 2 - 3 - 4 - 5**.
*   **Subdivisions**: [[Sixteenth_Notes|Sixteenth notes]] are counted as **1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 &**.
*   **Advanced Counting**: Once comfortable, drummers often count the pulses: **1 - 2 - 1 - 2 - 3** or **1 - 2 - 3 - 1 - 2**.

### [[Groove|Groove]] Application
When developing **[[5/8_Grooves]]**, the drummer should use the bass line or the "rhythmic template" of the composition as a guide. 
*   **One-Bar Patterns**: Focus on placing the snare or [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] on the start of the internal groupings (e.g., Bass on 1, Snare on 4 in a 3+2 feel).
*   **Two-Bar Patterns**: Often used to create a longer melodic phrase that resolves every two measures.

### Pro-Tip
In 5/8, every eighth note is technically a downbeat. Ensure your [[Hi-Hat|hi-hat]] or [[Ride_Cymbal|ride cymbal]] pattern reflects the specific grouping (2+3 or 3+2) to provide clarity for the rest of the ensemble.
