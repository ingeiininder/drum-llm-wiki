# Hi-Hat

The Hi-Hat consists of a pair of cymbals mounted on a metal stand and controlled by a foot pedal. It is the primary "engine" of the drum kit, used for keeping steady time and providing a wide range of articulation through pedal control.

## Pedal Control and Sound Textures

The dynamic range of the hi-hat is defined by how much pressure is applied to the foot pedal:

- **Tight/Closed Hi-Hat**: High pressure on the pedal creates a crisp, short [[Staccato|staccato]] sound. Ideal for verses or quiet sections where clarity is required.
- **Half-Open / Sloshy Hi-Hat**: Light pressure allows the top and bottom cymbals to vibrate against each other. This creates a thick, "sloshy" sound used in choruses or high-energy sections to drive the music.
- **[[Open_Hi-Hat|Open Hi-Hat]]**: Lifting the pedal entirely allows the cymbals to ring freely. Drummers often use quick **Hi-Hat Openings** on specific syncopated upbeats (e.g., the "&" of [[Beat|beat]] 4) to add texture.
- **Foot Splash/Chick**: Striking the cymbals together using only the foot pedal, without the use of sticks.

## Stylistic Applications

- **Jazz**: The hi-hat is traditionally played with the foot on **beats 2 and 4**, functioning as the "backbeat" alongside the ride cymbal pattern.
- **Rock and Pop**: Served as the main timekeeping surface. For a more aggressive sound, drummers use the **shoulder of the stick** to strike the edge of the hi-hats.
- **[[Bossa_Nova|Bossa Nova]]**: Typically plays consistent [[Eighth_Notes|eighth notes]] to provide a steady rhythmic anchor for Brazilian patterns.
- **[[Funk|Funk]]**: Focuses on syncopated hi-hat patterns with many [[Ghost_Notes|ghost notes]] and rapid openings.

## Technical Mastery

- **Marking the Form**: One of the most effective ways to mark a song's form is to switch playing surfaces. For example, playing the hi-hat during the "Verse" (Letter A) and switching to the **Ride Cymbal** for the "Chorus" (Letter B).
- **Stick Placement**: 
    - Using the **Tip** on the top of the cymbal yields a thin, "corny" sound.
    - Using the **Shoulder** just beneath the tip to strike the **Edge** yields a thick, dynamically controlled tone.
