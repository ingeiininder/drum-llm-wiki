# Heel Up Hi-Hat Method
The Heel Up Hi-Hat Method is the most widely used hi-hat stepping technique, offering the ability to play faster and with a bigger dynamic range compared to the [[Heel-Toe_Hi-Hat_Method]]. Unlike [[Bass Drum Technique]] (where the ankle is primary), this is primarily an upper leg technique, accessing the weight of the leg to produce a solid, consistent [[Hi-Hat "Chick" Sound]]. The primary motion originates from the hip.

## Execution Steps
1.  **Starting Position**: Begin with your heel up a few inches from the [[Hi-Hat Pedal]] plate, with your toes/ball of your foot resting towards the top of the pedal (Ex. 4.18).
2.  **Cymbal Separation**: Slightly raise your toes to separate the two hi-hat cymbals.
3.  **Downward Stroke**: Using the weight of your leg, play down through your heel and toes to produce a solid "chick" sound. The motion comes from the hip, driving the entire leg down.

## Characteristics and Applications
*   **Power and Dynamics**: This method allows for greater power and a wider dynamic range, making it suitable for louder musical contexts or when a strong, articulate "chick" is desired.
*   **Speed**: The larger muscle groups involved can facilitate faster, more continuous hi-hat patterns.
*   **Versatility**: It's highly versatile for various styles, from rock and funk to jazz.

## Achieving a Tight, Articulate "Chick" Sound
For both the heel/toe and heel-up methods, achieving a tight, articulate "chick" sound requires sufficient space between the two hi-hat cymbals. A minimum of one inch between the cymbals is necessary to produce this sound (Ex. 4.19). Anything less will compromise the sound quality (Ex. 4.20), resulting in a weaker or "fluffy" chick.

## Pro-Tips
*   **Hip-Driven Motion**: Focus on initiating the motion from your hip, allowing the weight of your entire leg to drive the pedal down.
*   **Relaxation**: While it's a powerful technique, maintain relaxation in your leg and foot to prevent fatigue and allow for fluid movement.
*   **Cymbal Spacing**: Regularly check your hi-hat cymbal spacing to ensure you're getting the best possible "chick" sound.
