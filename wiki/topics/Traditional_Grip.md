# Traditional Grip
Traditional Grip is a unique drumming technique that breaks from the [[Matched_Grip]] model by employing two distinct hand positions: an overhanded grip (generally [[American_Grip]] or [[German_Grip]]) for the right hand (for right-handed drummers) and an underhanded grip for the left hand.

## Historical Origin:
This technique originated with military marching drummers. In the past, drums were slung from the shoulder and positioned at an angle, closer to one hip, to make marching easier with a flat drum. This angled drum position necessitated an alteration in the left hand's grip to comfortably reach the drumhead, leading to the development of the underhanded traditional grip.

## Hand Positioning (Left Hand for Right-Handed Drummers):
1.  **Initial Position**: Position your left hand as if you were to use [[French_Grip]] (palm facing right, thumb up).
2.  **Stick Placement**: Place the stick on the webbing between your thumb and forefinger.
3.  **Ring and Pinkie Curl**: Curl your ring finger and pinkie underneath the stick so that the stick [[Rests|rests]] on the ring finger, close to the fingernail. These fingers provide the primary support and pivot.
4.  **Forefinger and Middle Finger Curl**: Curl your forefinger and middle finger over the top of the stick. These fingers provide control and articulation.

## Key Characteristics:
*   **Asymmetrical**: The most defining characteristic is the difference in grip between the two hands.
*   **Underhanded Left Hand**: The left hand's unique position allows for specific types of articulation and rebound.
*   **Sound and Feel**: Due to the different grips, achieving an equal sound and feel from both hands can be challenging and requires significant practice.

## Applications:
*   **[[Jazz|Jazz]]**: Historically and currently, a hallmark of [[Jazz|jazz]] drumming, particularly for [[Ride_Cymbal|ride cymbal]] patterns and [[Ghost_Notes|ghost notes]].
*   **[[Rudimental_Drumming|Rudimental Drumming]]**: Essential for many traditional rudimental [[Snare_Drum|snare drum]] techniques.
*   **Brush Playing**: Often preferred for brush work due to the natural angle it creates for sweeping motions.

## Pro-Tips:
*   **Dedicated Practice**: Developing proficiency in Traditional Grip requires more time and focused practice than matched grip techniques to achieve balance and fluidity between the hands.
*   **Listen and Observe**: Pay close attention to the sound and feel of each hand. Use a mirror or video to ensure proper hand positioning and stick motion.
*   **Validity**: Despite the initial challenges, Traditional Grip is a highly valid and expressive technique, as evidenced by its use by many of the greatest drummers throughout history.
