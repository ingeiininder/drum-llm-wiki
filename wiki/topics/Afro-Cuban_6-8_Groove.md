# Afro-Cuban 6/8 Groove

The **Afro-Cuban 6/8 Groove** is a foundational polyrhythmic structure in Latin percussion, characterized by its triplet-based feel and specific orchestration across the drum set. In a modern performance context, this groove is often led by a [[Cascara Pattern]] or a variation played on the [[Cowbell]] or ride cymbal bell.

### Orchestration and Structure
- **Ride Surface**: Typically played on a cowbell or the bell of the [[Ride Cymbal]]. The pattern maintains a constant dotted-quarter note pulse or a more syncopated Afro-Cuban bell pattern.
- **Snare Drum**: Often utilizes [[Cross-Stick]] patterns or high-tuned rimshots to emulate traditional percussion instruments like the Clave or Timbales.
- **Bass Drum**: Usually follows a "tumbao" influenced pattern, often emphasizing the "a" of beat 2 and beat 4 in a 6/8 context (the dotted quarter note pulse).
- **Toms**: Used to add melodic color, often toggling between the rack tom and floor tom to simulate the high and low heads of Congas.

### Performance Variations
- **Letter A/B Style**: A standard two-bar 6/8 pattern where the cowbell remains the primary timekeeper.
- **Letter A2 Variation**: To increase energy, the orchestration may shift to the [[Ride Cymbal]] while the bass part moves up an octave.
- **Modern Adaptation**: A variation used in contemporary settings involves a more "eighth-note-triplet" feel, making it applicable to jazz-fusion and modern world music.

### Pro-Tips
- **Phasing**: When playing a [[Pickup Fill]] into an Afro-Cuban section, ensure the fill is phrased in 6/8 to match the underlying subdivision.
- **Dynamic Control**: Use a "subtle" fill when transitioning back to a verse (Letter A) and a "forceful" fill when moving into a high-energy section like a solo or A2.
