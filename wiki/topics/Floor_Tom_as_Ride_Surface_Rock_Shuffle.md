# Floor Tom as Ride Surface (Rock Shuffle)
A focal point of the Unit 10 rock shuffle is the unique use of the [[Floor Tom]] as a primary ride surface, particularly in the [[Intro (Rock Shuffle - Unit 10)]]. This technique is employed to create a specific sonic texture.

**Performance Notes and Sound Goals:**
*   **Avoid Over-Striking**: It is "very important that you not strike the floor tom too hard." Hitting it too forcefully can result in an uncontrolled, boomy, or choked sound.
*   **Find the Sweet Spot**: The goal is to "find the sweet spot where the drum rumbles at an even keel without any notes standing out." This requires experimentation with stick placement (center, edge, halfway) and dynamic control.
    *   **Pro-Tip**: Often, playing slightly off-center or closer to the rim can produce a more controlled rumble than a direct center hit.
*   **Even Rumble**: The desired sound is a consistent, sustained "rumble" rather than distinct, sharp notes. This contributes to the "darker and ominous vibe" mentioned in the intro.
*   **Avoid Lopsided/Sloppy Sound**: If the sweet spot isn't achieved, or if dynamics are inconsistent, the groove will sound "lopsided and sloppy." This means the sustained quality will be uneven, and individual strokes might pop out too much.

**Challenge with Dynamics**:
*   This technique presents a significant challenge: while maintaining a medium, controlled volume on the floor tom for the ride pattern, you also need to hit the [[Snare Drum]] hard on beats 2 and 4 (as detailed in [[Snare Drum Loud on 2 and 4 (Rock Shuffle)]]). This requires excellent dynamic control and independence between your hands.

Mastering this technique is crucial for achieving the intended sonic character of the Unit 10 rock shuffle.
