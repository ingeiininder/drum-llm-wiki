# Burying the Beater
"Burying the beater" refers to the [[Bass_Drum_Technique|bass drum technique]] where the [[Bass_Drum_Beater]] is left pressed against the [[Bass_Drum_Head]] after it has been played. The alternative is to allow the beater to naturally rebound off the head after striking. This aspect of [[Bass_Drum_Technique|bass drum technique]] is often debated and primarily based on personal sonic preference.

## Sonic Impact
*   **Burying the Beater**: When the beater remains pressed against the head, it quickly stops the natural resonance of the [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]]. This produces a quick, dry, and muted sound, often favored in genres like [[Rock|rock]], metal, or [[Funk|funk]] where a punchy, less resonant [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] is desired.
*   **Not Burying the Beater**: Allowing the beater to rebound naturally off the head permits the [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] to resonate fully. This results in a more open, sustained, and resonant sound, often preferred in [[Jazz|jazz]], orchestral, or acoustic settings where the drum's natural tone is valued.

## Considerations
*   **Personal Preference**: The choice between burying or not burying the beater is largely a matter of the desired sound for a particular musical context.
*   **Control**: If you choose to bury the beater, it's crucial to pay attention to any residual "dribble" – a soft, unintended sound the beater might make if there isn't enough pressure holding it firmly against the head. This is rarely a desirable sound and should be controlled.
*   **Technique Integration**: The choice of beater control can be integrated with both [[Heel_Down_Bass_Drum_Technique]] and [[Heel_Up_Bass_Drum_Technique]].

## Pro-Tips
*   **Experiment**: Experiment with both approaches to understand how they affect the sound of your [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] in different musical situations.
*   **Listen Critically**: Record yourself and listen back to discern the subtle differences in sustain, attack, and overall tone.
*   **Dynamic Control**: Develop the ability to control the beater's contact with the head precisely, whether you choose to bury it or let it rebound, to achieve your desired sound consistently.
