# Phrasing

Phrasing (or [[Phrase_Interpretation|Phrase Interpretation]]) relates to the manner in which individual notes of a particular group are actually played. It is the bridge between literal rhythmic reading and artistic musicality. For a drummer, phrasing involves making choices about sound, [[Accents|accents]], and orchestration to support the structural arc of a song.

## Structural Elements of Phrasing

Phrasing is often defined by three key musical attributes:
- **[[Groove|Groove]] Choice**: Selecting the right feel (e.g., straight vs. swung) to match the ensemble.
- **Section Length**: Recognizing the natural "sentence" of the music, which commonly spans lengths of 4, 8, 10, or 12 bars.
- **Riff and Chord Structure**: Tailoring the drum parts to follow the rhythmic logic of the guitar, bass, or ensemble figures.

## Types of Phrasing

- **[[Swung_16th_Phrasing|Swung 16th Phrasing]]**: Interpreting notation written as straight [[16th_Notes|16th notes]] as if they are part of a 16th-note triplet (removing the middle note).
- **[[Jazz|Jazz]] Phrasing**: A specialized context where even [[Eighth_Notes|eighth notes]] are interpreted as swung eighths. In this style, the **"Eighth-Note Rule"** suggests that a freestanding eighth note with nothing following it should be accented.
- **[[Odd_Meter|Odd Meter]] Phrasing**: Grouping beats in odd signatures to create a flow. For example, **7/8** can be phrased as 2+2+3 or 3+2+2 to accommodate different rhythmic patterns.
- **Over-the-Bar Phrasing**: Rhythmic patterns that cross bar lines (such as dotted quarter figures in [[Jazz|jazz]]), creating a grouping of three over four.

## Professional Phrasing Strategies

- **Setting Up Figures**: Using fills to "set up" or signal an upcoming ensemble figure. A common professional guideline is that **musicality must supersede the desire to over-[[Fill|fill]]**; drummers must detect places where little to no set-up is needed to avoid "clouding" the figure.
- **Matching Note Lengths**: Identifying whether a written figure is "short" or "long" and orchestrating it on the appropriate surface (e.g., snare vs. crash cymbals) to match the band.
- **Resetting with Fills**: Using a clear [[Fill|fill]] at the end of a complex phrase to help the band and the drummer "reset" and recover from tension-heavy patterns.
