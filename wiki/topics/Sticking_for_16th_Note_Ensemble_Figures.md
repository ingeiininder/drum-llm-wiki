# Sticking for 16th-Note Ensemble Figures

When executing [[Set-up Fill|Set-up Fills]] for 16th-note [[Ensemble Figure|Ensemble Figures]], sticking choices are dictated by two main factors: physical comfort and the "destination" sound source.

### Primary Sticking Options
1. **Hand-to-Hand (Alternating)**:
    *   *Pros*: Maintains a natural flow and consistent 16th-note pulse.
    *   *Cons*: Can sometimes leave the "wrong" hand available for the final accent or cymbal crash.
2. **Double Strokes**:
    *   *Pros*: Allows the drummer to manipulate which hand ends the fill, facilitating easier movement to specific cymbals (e.g., a China cymbal on the left).
    *   *Cons*: Requires higher technical control to maintain the same volume and "weight" as single strokes.

### Strategic Considerations
*   **Destination**: Where do you need to be after the figure? If you need to immediately resume a hi-hat [[Groove]] with your right hand, you may want to end the ensemble figure with your left hand or use a sticking that frees the right hand early.
*   **Orchestration**: If the figure requires a move from the snare to a floor tom, choose a sticking that avoids crossing your arms.
*   **Reversibility**: All standard sticking patterns (e.g., R L R L) should be practiced starting with the opposite hand to maximize flexibility.

### Example Sticking (The "e" of Beat 1)
*   **Hand-to-Hand**: R L [R] (where [R] is the ensemble figure).
*   **With Doubles**: R L R R [L] (allows the left hand to take the figure).
