# 16th-Note [[Ensemble_Figures|Ensemble Figures]]

In genres such as **[[Funk|Funk]]**, **R&B**, and **[[Rock|Rock]]**, [[Ensemble_Figures|ensemble figures]] are frequently based on [[16th-Note_Subdivisions]]. These figures are often highly syncopated, focusing on the "e" and "ah" (the second and fourth partials) of the [[Beat|beat]].

### The Importance of [[Subdividing|Subdividing]]
To accurately place these figures, the drummer must internalize the 16th-note grid. If the internal clock is only feeling [[Eighth_Notes|eighth notes]], the "e" and "ah" will often feel "off-balance" or rushed.

### Technical Execution Methods
When practicing these figures, Musicians Institute recommends three distinct [[Hi-Hat]] templates:
1.  **[[One-Handed_16ths|One-Handed 16ths]]**: Best for medium tempos (approx. 75 BPM).
2.  **[[Two-Handed_16ths|Two-Handed 16ths]] (Alternating)**: Best for higher energy or faster tempos (approx. 90 BPM).
3.  **[[Eighth_Notes|Eighth Notes]]**: A standard [[Rock|rock]]/pop approach (approx. 85 BPM).

### Key Concepts
- **[[Recovery_Action]]**: The ability to land precisely on the following downbeat after a syncopated "e" or "ah" hit.
- **[[The_Push]]**: Rhythmic anticipation where a figure on the "ah" makes the following downbeat feel as if it arrived early.
