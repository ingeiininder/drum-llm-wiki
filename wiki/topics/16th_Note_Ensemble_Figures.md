# 16th-Note Ensemble Figures

In genres such as **Funk**, **R&B**, and **Rock**, ensemble figures are frequently based on [[16th-Note Subdivisions]]. These figures are often highly syncopated, focusing on the "e" and "ah" (the second and fourth partials) of the beat.

### The Importance of Subdividing
To accurately place these figures, the drummer must internalize the 16th-note grid. If the internal clock is only feeling eighth notes, the "e" and "ah" will often feel "off-balance" or rushed.

### Technical Execution Methods
When practicing these figures, Musicians Institute recommends three distinct [[Hi-Hat]] templates:
1.  **One-Handed 16ths**: Best for medium tempos (approx. 75 BPM).
2.  **Two-Handed 16ths (Alternating)**: Best for higher energy or faster tempos (approx. 90 BPM).
3.  **Eighth Notes**: A standard rock/pop approach (approx. 85 BPM).

### Key Concepts
- **[[Recovery Action]]**: The ability to land precisely on the following downbeat after a syncopated "e" or "ah" hit.
- **[[The Push]]**: Rhythmic anticipation where a figure on the "ah" makes the following downbeat feel as if it arrived early.
