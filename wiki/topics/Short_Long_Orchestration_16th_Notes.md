# Short/Long Orchestration (16th Notes)

This orchestration method is designed to help drummers interpret [[Ensemble Figures]] based on 16th-note rhythms. By categorizing notes by their duration, the drummer can create a musical distinction between "short" staccato hits and "long" sustained or emphasized tones.

### The Ostinato
While reading the rhythmic figures, maintain a consistent pulse:
*   **Ride Cymbal**: Continual 16th notes.
*   **Hi-Hat (Foot)**: Quarter notes on beats 1, 2, 3, and 4.

### Orchestration Rules
The following rules apply to the interpretation of the written rhythmic staff:
1.  **Long Notes**: Any note value equal to a **dotted eighth note or larger** is played on the [[Bass Drum]].
2.  **Short Notes**: Any note value equal to an **eighth note or shorter** is played on the [[Snare Drum]].

### Pro-Tip
When executing "Long" notes on the bass drum, ensure the ride cymbal note aligns perfectly with the kick to provide a "thick" ensemble sound. For "Short" notes, keep the snare drum accents crisp and avoid letting the stick linger on the head, maintaining the staccato quality.

### Common Pitfalls
*   **Flamming**: Ensure the ride cymbal and the orchestrated drum (snare or bass) strike simultaneously.
*   **Losing the Pulse**: Beginners often let the hi-hat foot waver when the reading becomes syncopated. Practice with a [[Metronome]] at 75 BPM as suggested in [[Exercise 9.9]].
