# Short/Long Orchestration ([[16th_Notes|16th Notes]])

This orchestration method is designed to help drummers interpret [[Ensemble_Figures]] based on 16th-note rhythms. By categorizing notes by their [[Duration|duration]], the drummer can create a musical distinction between "short" [[Staccato|staccato]] hits and "long" sustained or emphasized tones.

### The Ostinato
While reading the rhythmic figures, maintain a consistent [[Pulse|pulse]]:
*   **[[Ride_Cymbal|Ride Cymbal]]**: Continual [[16th_Notes|16th notes]].
*   **[[Hi-Hat|Hi-Hat]] (Foot)**: [[Quarter_Notes|Quarter notes]] on beats 1, 2, 3, and 4.

### Orchestration Rules
The following rules apply to the interpretation of the written rhythmic staff:
1.  **Long Notes**: Any [[Note_Value|note value]] equal to a **[[Dotted_Eighth_Note|dotted eighth note]] or larger** is played on the [[Bass_Drum]].
2.  **Short Notes**: Any [[Note_Value|note value]] equal to an **eighth note or shorter** is played on the [[Snare_Drum]].

### Pro-Tip
When executing "Long" notes on the bass drum, ensure the [[Ride_Cymbal|ride cymbal]] note aligns perfectly with the kick to provide a "thick" ensemble sound. For "Short" notes, keep the snare drum [[Accents|accents]] crisp and avoid letting the stick linger on the head, maintaining the [[Staccato|staccato]] quality.

### Common Pitfalls
*   **Flamming**: Ensure the [[Ride_Cymbal|ride cymbal]] and the orchestrated drum (snare or bass) strike simultaneously.
*   **Losing the [[Pulse|Pulse]]**: Beginners often let the [[Hi-Hat|hi-hat]] foot waver when the reading becomes syncopated. Practice with a [[Metronome]] at 75 BPM as suggested in [[Exercise_9.9]].
