# Brush Technique: The Conventional Stroke

The **Conventional Stroke** is the standard method for timekeeping with brushes in medium to up-tempo jazz contexts. It mimics the rhythmic structure and "lilt" of the conventional [[Jazz Ride Pattern]].

### The Right Hand (Rhythmic Lead)
The right hand provides the articulated "ping" sound of the ride pattern.
- **Motion**: Start at the "tail" of the stroke, using a broken triplet as a pickup.
- **Target**: Move to the "point" on beat 1, lift, and return to the tail for beat 2. 
- **Rhythm**: It follows the standard "1, 2-ah, 3, 4-ah" jazz rhythm.

### The Left Hand (The Sweep)
The left hand provides the "sustained" surface noise (the "stir") that defines brush playing.
- **Motion**: Continuous clockwise circles.
- **Execution**: Start at the tail and move toward the point for beat 1. Complete the circle by gently sweeping back to the tail by beat 2.
- **Pressure**: Avoid "digging in" or pressing too hard on the drumhead, which can choke the sound.

### Coordination and Feel
- **Hi-Hat**: The hi-hat should close on beats 2 and 4.
- **Synchronization**: The hands should come together on beats 2 and 4 to reinforce the backbeat.
- **Pro-Tip**: Adding a slight accent with the left-hand sweep on beats 2 and 4 helps convey a stronger time feel and assists the bass player in finding the "pocket."
