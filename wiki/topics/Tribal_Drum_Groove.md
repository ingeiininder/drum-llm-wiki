# Tribal Drum Groove

A "Tribal" groove in a modern drumming context refers to a feel that emphasizes [[Toms]] and percussive textures over the standard snare-heavy backbeat. This style is often used to create a primal, driving atmosphere in verses or atmospheric sections of a song.

### Key Characteristics
- **Snares Off**: The [[Snare Drum]] is often played with the snares disengaged to mimic the sound of a high tom or a field drum.
- **Rim Textures**: Incorporating [[Tom Rims]] or "stick-on-stick" clicks to add "random" percussive elements.
- **Constant Pulse**: Often supported by a [[Shaker]] or a steady eighth-note/quarter-note pulse on a floor tom or bass drum.
- **Orchestration**: The groove is distributed across the kit, focusing on the lower frequencies of the toms to create a "thick" sonic landscape.

### Professional Context: "Anything" (Todd Sucherman)
In the track "Anything," Todd Sucherman utilizes a tribal groove to react emotionally to the lyrics. He creates "scene changes" by switching from this tribal tom-based feel in the verses to a conventional [[Ride Cymbal]] and snare-on groove in the choruses.

### Pro-Tips
- **Dynamics**: Use the tribal feel to "pull back" the energy, allowing the chorus to have a more dramatic "payoff" when the snares are engaged.
- **Notation**: In professional charts, x-shaped note heads on tom lines typically indicate playing on the [[Tom Rims]].
