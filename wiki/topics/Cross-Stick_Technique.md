# Cross-Stick Technique

The cross-stick (sometimes called "rim click" or "side stick") is a fundamental [[Snare Drum]] articulation used to produce a strong, full, and warm percussive sound, often employed in jazz, R&B, ballads, and acoustic settings where a full snare hit would be too loud.

## Execution

1.  **Stick Position**: Turn the stick around and use the butt end. The bead (tip) of your stick should be placed just "off-center," typically between the 8 and 9 o'clock position on the drum head. The butt end of the stick will be off the drum, between the 3 and 4 o'clock position, resting on the rim.
2.  **Grip and Stroke**: Grip the stick in your normal fashion. To make the stroke, "throw" the stick into the drum, allowing your four fingers to come off the stick. At this point, your fingers should flatten, and you will apply pressure to keep the stick firmly on both the [[Drum Head]] and the [[Drum Rim]].
3.  **Repeat**: To make the next cross-stick stroke, re-grab the stick with your fingers and repeat the motion.

## Finding the "Sweet Spot"

While the general positioning described above will help you achieve a solid cross-stick sound, each [[Snare Drum]] is unique and has its own "sweet spot." It is extremely important to experiment with slight variations in stick placement on both the head and the rim to find the position that produces the best, most resonant sound for that particular drum.

## Pro-Tips

*   **Dynamics**: The amount of pressure applied with your palm and fingers directly affects the volume and sustain of the cross-stick. Experiment with lighter and heavier pressure for different dynamic effects.
*   **Consistency**: Practice maintaining a consistent sound and volume, especially when playing repetitive patterns.
*   **Rim Contact**: Ensure good contact between the stick and the rim. A loose contact will result in a weak or choked sound.
*   **Butt End**: Using the butt end of the stick provides more mass and surface area, contributing to a fuller, warmer tone compared to using the stick's tip.
*   **Applications**: Master this technique for playing softer grooves, accompanying vocals, or creating a different texture in your drumming.
