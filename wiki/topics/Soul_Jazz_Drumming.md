# Soul-[[Jazz|Jazz]] Drumming

Soul-[[Jazz|Jazz]] emerged in the mid-1950s (notably with organist **Jimmy Smith**) as a reaction against the complex chord changes and "walking" bass lines of Bebop. It focuses on syncopated grooves, repetitive bass lines, and minimal chord changes.

### Stylistic Influences
The style is defined by the "funky soul swamp" drumming of pioneers such as:
- [[Idris_Muhammad]]
- [[Bill_Stewart]]
- [[Jabo_Starks]]
- [[Clyde_Stubblefield]]

### Technical Characteristics
- **[[Groove|Groove]]-Centric**: The drummer crafts accompaniment around recurring bass motifs rather than constant melodic interaction.
- **Straight 16th Feel**: Unlike traditional swing, many Soul-[[Jazz|Jazz]] tracks utilize a [[Straight_16th-Note_Funk]] feel.
- **Interaction**: In sections like the "Guitar/Drum Duet" (Ex 1.7), the drummer must "vibe" and respond to the soloist, moving beyond a static pattern into a "[[Groove|groove]] solo" context.

### Sound and Articulation
To achieve an authentic Soul-[[Jazz|Jazz]] sound, specific [[Drumset_Articulation]] is required:
1. **Open [[Bass_Drum|Bass Drum]]**: Use a "non-buried" beater technique to mimic the small, legato bass drums of the [[Jazz|jazz]] tradition.
2. **Light Rim Shots**: Snare [[Accents|accents]] should be used to make the drum "speak" rather than for sheer volume.
3. **Loose Hi-Hats**: Avoid a "clean" pop sound. A slightly open, "dirty" [[Hi-Hat|hi-hat]] sound reflects the New Orleans and early R&B influence.
