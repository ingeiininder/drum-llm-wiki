# 32nd-Note Ensemble Figures

In advanced [[Drum Reading]], [[32nd Notes]] are frequently utilized to "fill in" the gaps between [[16th_Note]] ensemble figures. This technique creates a dense, driving rhythmic texture often found in modern big band or fusion settings.

### Application in Four-Bar Phrases
A standard practice method involves playing three bars of "time" (a consistent [[Groove]]) followed by a fourth bar containing a specific ensemble figure. 
1. **The Figure**: Read the written 16th-note rhythm.
2. **The Fill-In**: Use 32nd notes to connect the rhythmic hits of the figure.
3. **The Exit**: Execute a [[Fill]] of your choice when transitioning out of the fourth bar back into the groove.

### Orchestration Strategies
When interpreting these figures, utilize various [[Orchestration]] ideas to move the 32nd notes around the kit:
- **Snare to Toms**: Moving linear patterns across the drums.
- **Accented Figures**: Crashing on the primary ensemble hits while keeping the 32nd-note "fill-ins" at a lower dynamic level ([[Ghost Notes]]).

> [!PRO-TIP]
> To maintain clarity at high speeds, ensure your 32nd notes are played with a relaxed [[Moeller Technique]] or controlled finger strokes. If the 32nd notes are too loud, they will obscure the actual ensemble figure you are trying to support.

### 16-Bar Phrasing
To develop endurance and fluency, practice 16-bar phrases that alternate between extended periods of time-keeping and complex 32nd-note figures. This simulates real-world chart reading where figures may appear sporadically or in long sequences.
