# Lead Sheet Interpretation (Drumset)

A **Lead Sheet** is a skeletal sketch of a composition, typically providing the melody, chord structure, and form. Unlike a specific drum chart, it requires the drummer to use professional intuition to create a part.

### Core Principles of Interpretation
- **Feel Creation**: The lead sheet does not contain "beats." The drummer must choose the appropriate feel (e.g., [[Soul-Jazz Drumming]], [[New Orleans Second Line]], or [[Odd Time Funk]]) based on the style marking.
- **Selective Reading**: It is neither necessary nor desirable to play every rhythm written on the lead sheet. The drummer should play "time" through most figures and only "catch" or mark the most important ensemble figures.
- **Marking the Form**: The drummer's primary job is to signal transitions between sections (Intro, A, B, Coda) using [[Fill Markers]] and dynamic shifts.
- **Orchestration**: Drummers must decide which sound sources (e.g., [[Ghost Notes]] on snare vs. ride bell) best fit the short or long note durations indicated in the melody.

### Sectional Navigation
- **Open Vamps**: Sections marked "Vamp Till Cue" require the drummer to maintain a consistent groove while watching the bandleader (or soloist) for the transition signal.
- **D.S. al Coda**: Standard navigation where the drummer returns to the "Sign" (Segno) and plays until the Coda jump.

> [!tip] Pro-Tip
> When a lead sheet shows a unison melody with short notes, orchestrate your response on "short" sound sources like the snare or high toms to match the band's articulation.
