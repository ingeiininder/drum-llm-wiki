# Progressive Metal Foundations

[[Progressive_Metal_Drumming|Progressive Metal drumming]] requires a fusion of high-intensity execution and complex rhythmic architecture. Within the Musicians Institute curriculum, this style is introduced through the study of [[Double_Bass_Drumming]] consistency, [[Odd_Groupings]], and [[Over-the-Bar_Phrases]].

### Key Technical Elements
- **16th-Note Groupings of Three**: A common motif in the genre involves [[Phrasing|phrasing]] [[16th_Notes|16th notes]] in groups of three (Ex 10.3), which creates a natural hemiola effect against a standard 4/4 [[Meter|meter]].
- **Double Bass Consistency**: The standard 16th-note double bass pattern (Ex 10.5) serves as a diagnostic tool for the drummer's [[Throne_Balance]]. 
- **Backbeat Execution**: In this context, the [[Snare_Drum|snare drum]] typically delivers a heavy [[Rimshot]] on beats 2 and 4 to provide a stable anchor for the complex [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] figures.
- **Cymbal Articulation**: The ride surface often shifts to a crash cymbal playing [[Quarter_Notes|quarter notes]]. These must be powerful and consistent to match the high energy of the genre.

### Performance Notes
- **The "Ah" Accent**: Phrases often conclude on the "ah" of a [[Beat|beat]] (e.g., the end of an A-section), requiring a sharp accent and an immediate cutoff to leave subsequent measures silent.
- **[[Syncopation|Syncopation]]**: Advanced sections (Letter B) utilize highly syncopated [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] patterns featuring "e's" and "ah's" to create tension against a steady [[Hi-Hat]] eighth-note [[Pulse|pulse]].

> [!tip] Pro-Tip
> When playing heavy crash-ride [[Quarter_Notes|quarter notes]], ensure you are hitting the edge of the cymbal with a glancing blow to prevent cracking while maintaining maximum volume and "wash."
