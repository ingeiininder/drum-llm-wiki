# Progressive Metal Foundations

Progressive Metal drumming requires a fusion of high-intensity execution and complex rhythmic architecture. Within the Musicians Institute curriculum, this style is introduced through the study of [[Double Bass Drumming]] consistency, [[Odd Groupings]], and [[Over-the-Bar Phrases]].

### Key Technical Elements
- **16th-Note Groupings of Three**: A common motif in the genre involves phrasing 16th notes in groups of three (Ex 10.3), which creates a natural hemiola effect against a standard 4/4 meter.
- **Double Bass Consistency**: The standard 16th-note double bass pattern (Ex 10.5) serves as a diagnostic tool for the drummer's [[Throne Balance]]. 
- **Backbeat Execution**: In this context, the snare drum typically delivers a heavy [[Rimshot]] on beats 2 and 4 to provide a stable anchor for the complex bass drum figures.
- **Cymbal Articulation**: The ride surface often shifts to a crash cymbal playing quarter notes. These must be powerful and consistent to match the high energy of the genre.

### Performance Notes
- **The "Ah" Accent**: Phrases often conclude on the "ah" of a beat (e.g., the end of an A-section), requiring a sharp accent and an immediate cutoff to leave subsequent measures silent.
- **Syncopation**: Advanced sections (Letter B) utilize highly syncopated bass drum patterns featuring "e's" and "ah's" to create tension against a steady [[Hi-Hat]] eighth-note pulse.

> [!tip] Pro-Tip
> When playing heavy crash-ride quarter notes, ensure you are hitting the edge of the cymbal with a glancing blow to prevent cracking while maintaining maximum volume and "wash."
