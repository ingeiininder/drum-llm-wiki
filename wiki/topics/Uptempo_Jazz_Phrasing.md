# Uptempo Jazz Phrasing

Playing at fast tempos (e.g., $J = 240+$ BPM) requires a shift in both physical approach and musical philosophy. The goal is to maintain a "floating" feel rather than a "driving" or "heavy" one.

### Technical Adjustments
- **Stick Work**: At high speeds, the drummer must let the stick do more of the work. Utilize the natural rebound of the [[Ride Cymbal]] and minimize large arm movements.
- **Legato Strokes**: Think of a flowing, connected motion. Avoid "stiff" or "vertical" strokes which can make the time feel forced.
- **Shouldered Cymbal**: For long, legato accents (especially in intros), use the shoulder of the stick against the bow of the cymbal. This produces a washier, broader sound than a tip-hit or a sharp edge-crash.

### Dynamic Shaping
- **Ascending/Descending Lines**: A professional technique is to follow the melodic contour of the soloists. As a soloist's line ascends in pitch/intensity, increase the drum volume; as it descends, decrease the volume.
- **Comping**: In uptempo settings, "less is more." Comping too busily can clutter the frequency space and make it difficult for the soloist to navigate.

### Structural Phrasing
- **Trading Fours**: The final four-bar section of a [[Trading Fours]] sequence should be phrased decisively to signal the return to the "Head" (the main melody).
- **The Ending**: A common jazz ending involves a light cymbal crash, followed by a volume swell (tremolo), a decrescendo, and a final light crash with a bass drum note.
