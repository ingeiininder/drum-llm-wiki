# Short vs Long Note Interpretation

In [[Ensemble_Reading]], drummers must decide how to orchestrate rhythmic figures based on their [[Duration|duration]]. This is known as **[[Note_Length_Interpretation|Note Length Interpretation]]**. Assigning specific drum set voices to "short" and "long" notes helps the drummer support the horn section or melody more effectively.

### Orchestration Rules (Ex 9.5)
When reading a rhythmic staff, apply the following logic:

1. **Short Notes**: 
   - **Definition**: Any note equal to an eighth note or shorter (e.g., 8th notes, [[16th_Notes|16th notes]]).
   - **Voice**: Play on the **[[Snare_Drum|Snare Drum]]**.
   - **Character**: [[Staccato|Staccato]], crisp, and immediate.

2. **Long Notes**:
   - **Definition**: Any note longer than an eighth note (e.g., [[Quarter_Notes|Quarter notes]], Dotted Quarters, Half notes, Whole notes, or tied notes).
   - **Voice**: Play on the **[[Bass_Drum|Bass Drum]]** (often reinforced with a cymbal crash).
   - **Character**: Sustained, heavy, and resonant.

### The [[Hi-Hat|Hi-Hat]]/Ride Anchor
While interpreting these figures, maintain a consistent ostinato:
- **[[Ride_Cymbal|Ride Cymbal]]**: Continual [[Eighth_Notes|eighth notes]].
- **[[Hi-Hat|Hi-Hat]] Foot**: [[Quarter_Notes|Quarter notes]] on all four beats.

### Application
This technique is the foundation for [[Big_Band_Drumming]] and modern [[Chart|chart]] reading. It allows the drummer to "color" the rhythm, making the drum part sound like a musical arrangement rather than just a series of hits.
