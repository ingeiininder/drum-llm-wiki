# Symmetrical vs. Asymmetrical Time Signatures

In percussion performance and reading, time signatures are categorized based on how their internal beats are divided and felt.

### Symmetrical Time Signatures
Symmetrical meters (often called "common" or "regular" meters) are those where each measure can be evenly divided into equal halves. These are the standard foundations of most popular music.
*   **4/4 Time**: Divided into two equal halves of two beats each (two half notes).
*   **3/4 and 6/8 Time**: While 3 is an odd number, these are considered symmetrical because they can be divided into two equal pulses (e.g., two dotted quarter notes in 6/8).
*   **2/4 Time**: Divided into two equal quarter-note pulses.

### Asymmetrical Time Signatures
Asymmetrical meters, commonly referred to as **[[Odd Meter]]**, cannot be divided into equal halves. While they may contain two or three dominant beats (pulses) per measure, these pulses are of unequal value.
*   **Example (5/8)**: A measure of 5/8 contains five eighth notes. It is typically felt as two dominant pulses, but one pulse consists of three eighth notes and the other consists of two (3+2 or 2+3).

### Pro-Tip: The "Dominant Beat" Concept
The feel of an asymmetrical meter is defined by its **[[Asymmetrical Groupings]]**. Identifying where the "long" and "short" beats fall is essential for maintaining the [[Groove]] and locking in with the rhythm section.
