# Syncopation

Syncopation is a rhythmic technique that emphasizes the weaker beats or upbeats (such as the "&s" and "ahs" of a [[Subdivision|subdivision]]) rather than the standard downbeats. It is a defining element of modern drumming, providing forward motion and a "fat" feel to the [[Groove|groove]].

## Core Concepts

- **Upbeat Emphasis**: While basic drumming often places [[Quarter_Notes|quarter notes]] on downbeats, syncopation shifts these [[Accents|accents]] to the upbeats.
- **[[Rhythmic_Illusion|Rhythmic Illusion]]**: Specific performance methods, such as striking the edge of the [[Hi-Hat|hi-hat]], can create a rhythmically thick and [[Tempo|tempo]]-based illusion, enhancing the forward motion of a [[Groove|groove]].
- **Pushed Syncopation**: Also known as "Pushes" or anticipated figures, this occurs when an ensemble plays a chord on the "ah" of 4 instead of the following downbeat.

## Application on the Drum Set

- **[[Bass_Drum_Syncopation|Bass Drum Syncopation]]**: Applying [[Syncopated_Rhythms|syncopated rhythms]] to the [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] while maintaining a steady [[Hi-Hat|hi-hat]] or [[Ride_Cymbal|ride cymbal]] ostinato.
- **Interplay**: Establishing complex interactions between the [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] and [[Snare_Drum|snare drum]], such as the [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] "catching" [[Timekeeping_Figures|timekeeping figures]] while the [[Snare_Drum|snare drum]] remains on beats 2 and 4.
- **Rudimental Syncopation**: Utilizing 16th-note variations of [[Rudiments|rudiments]] (like the [[Paradiddle|paradiddle]] or rolls) to offer different syncopated textures.

## Key References
- **Syncopation by Ted Reed**: A foundational text mentioned within the curriculum for developing rhythmic independence and syncopated [[Phrasing|phrasing]].
