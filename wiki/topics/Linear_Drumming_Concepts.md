# [[Linear_Drumming|Linear Drumming]] Concepts
**[[Linear_Drumming|Linear Drumming]]** is defined by a texture where no two voices (limbs) sound simultaneously. This creates a "stream" of notes that can be highly syncopated and melodic.

### The Linear Template
In a standard [[Rock|rock]] [[Beat|beat]], the hi-hat often plays simultaneously with the snare or [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]]. In a linear version, these "stacked" notes are removed.
- **Example**: Removing the hi-hat from the downbeats of a [[Groove|groove]], leaving only the upbeats and the backbeat, creates a basic linear feel.

### Mental [[Subdivision|Subdivision]]
Because there is no constant [[Hi-Hat]] "time-keeper" ([[Subdivision|subdivision]] template), the player must have a very strong internal clock.
- **The 16th-Note Trainer**: A fundamental exercise involves playing a repeating 3-note pattern (e.g., Bass-Hat-Snare) across 16th-note subdivisions. Because 3 does not divide evenly into 16, the pattern "rotates" through the [[Measure|measure]].

### Practice Strategies
1. **Feeling vs. Hearing**: Focus on the 16th-note "grid" rather than the melody of the pattern.
2. **[[Counting_Out_Loud|Counting Out Loud]]**: Count [[Quarter_Notes|quarter notes]] (1, 2, 3, 4) while playing complex linear patterns to ensure the downbeats remain anchored.
3. **Accenting Downbeats**: Play the note that falls on the "1, 2, 3, 4" louder to solidify the [[Pulse|pulse]].

### Pro-Tips
- **Turnarounds**: When practicing repeating 3-note linear patterns, use a "turnaround" (an extra [[Bass_Drum|bass drum]] note or snare hit) at the end of a 4-bar phrase to reset the pattern to the downbeat.
- **[[Dynamics|Dynamics]]**: [[Linear_Drumming|Linear drumming]] relies heavily on the contrast between [[Accents_Drumming]] and [[Ghost_Notes]] to create a "[[Groove|groove]]" rather than just a string of notes.
