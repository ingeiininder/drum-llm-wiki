# Cut Time Drum Set Interpretation

In professional drum charts, [[Cut Time]] (2/2) is the standard notation for high-energy Latin, Afro-Cuban, and certain Caribbean styles. Interpreting these charts requires the drummer to translate simple rhythmic figures into stylistic [[Groove]] patterns and [[Orchestration|orchestrated]] fills.

### Stylistic Applications

#### 1. Mambo
In a [[Mambo]] chart, the 2/2 signature supports the "two-feel" of the bass line. 
- **Interpretation**: The drummer often plays a 2-bar bell pattern. Written ensemble figures in the chart are typically "set up" with short fills that lead into the hits.

#### 2. Samba
[[Samba]] is almost always felt in two. 
- **Interpretation**: The bass drum maintains a consistent "surdo" feel (usually emphasizing the second beat of the cut-time measure). Written figures are often interpreted as syncopated snare or rim-click accents.

#### 3. Reggae (One-Drop)
While [[Reggae]] is often written in 4/4, it is frequently notated in Cut Time to emphasize the "One-Drop" feel.
- **Interpretation**: In a One-Drop, the "1" of the cut-time measure is left empty, and the "2" is played with a simultaneous Snare/Bass drum hit.
- **Four-on-the-Floor**: Some reggae variations use a steady quarter-note bass drum (which appears as 8th notes in Cut Time).

### Ensemble Figures and Set-ups
When a chart in Cut Time shows an ensemble figure (e.g., an accented 8th note on the "&" of 2):
1. **Identify the Hit**: Determine where the figure falls within the 2-beat measure.
2. **The Set-up**: Play a fill that ends exactly before the hit. In Cut Time, these fills often utilize [[Triplets]] or [[16th Notes]] (which feel like 32nd notes in 4/4).

> [!PRO-TIP]
> When reading Cut Time, look at the "big picture." Don't get bogged down in the individual 8th notes; focus on the two main pulses per bar to keep the groove from rushing.
