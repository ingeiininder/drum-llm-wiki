# Cross-Stick Technique

The **Cross-Stick** (sometimes called "rim click" or "side stick") is a fundamental [[Snare_Drum]] articulation used to produce a strong, full, and warm percussive sound. It is frequently employed in [[Jazz|Jazz]], Bossa Nova, R&B, ballads, and acoustic settings where a full snare backbeat would be too intrusive.

## Execution and Position

1.  **Stick Orientation**: Turn the stick around and use the **butt end** for a fuller, warmer tone.
2.  **Bead Placement**: Place the tip (bead) of the stick on the head, typically between the **8 and 9 o'clock** position.
3.  **Rim Contact**: The butt end of the stick should extend over the rim, resting between the **2 and 4 o'clock** position.
4.  **The Stroke**: Grip the stick normally, then "throw" the butt end into the rim. Upon impact, flatten your fingers to apply pressure, keeping the stick in contact with the head to muffle unwanted overtones.

## Finding the "Sweet Spot"

Every snare drum is unique and has its own "sweet spot." It is extremely important to experiment with the ratio of "on-drum" vs. "off-drum" stick length. Finding that precise position on the rim and head that produces the most resonant, "woody" sound is a key skill for a professional drummer.

## Pro-Tips

*   **[[Dynamics|Dynamics]]**: The amount of pressure applied with your palm and fingers directly affects the volume and sustain. Experiment with lighter and heavier pressure for different musical textures.
*   **Visual Markers**: For a consistent sound, try to hit exactly the same spot on the rim every time. Some drummers mark their "sweet spot" with a small piece of tape or a pencil mark.
*   **Muffling**: Ensure your palm stays in contact with the drum head. A loose contact will result in a weak, thin, or "choked" sound.
