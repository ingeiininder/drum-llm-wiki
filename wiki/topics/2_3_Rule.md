# 2/3 Rule
The **2/3 Rule** is a common practice in musical notation, particularly in [[4-4_Time_Signature]], designed to enhance readability. It dictates that the [[Downbeat]] of beat 3 should not be obscured by a single note that spans across it.

**Core Principle:**
In 4/4 time, a measure is conceptually split into two sections of two beats each (beats 1-2 and beats 3-4). An "imaginary bar line" exists between beats 2 and 3. To maintain clarity, a note should not begin before beat 3 and extend past it, or begin before beat 3 and cover it entirely, if it can be broken up with a [[Ties]].

**Practical Application:**
*   While it's acceptable to place [[Half Note]]s on beats 1 and 3, a half note *cannot* be placed on beat 2, as it would obscure the downbeat of beat 3.
*   Instead, to represent a sound lasting from beat 2 through beat 3, [[Ties]] are used: a [[Quarter Note]] on beat 2 tied to a quarter note on beat 3. This clearly shows the attack on beat 2 and the downbeat of beat 3.

**Pro-Tip:** Adhering to the 2/3 rule makes rhythmic patterns much easier to read at a glance, especially in [[Sight-Reading]] situations. While you may encounter charts from inexperienced composers that break this rule, understanding it allows you to interpret the rhythm correctly and even mentally re-notate it for clarity. This principle extends to [[Syncopated Rhythms]] involving [[Eighth Notes]] and [[Sixteenth Notes]] where individual beat downbeats must remain clear.
