# Fill

A [[Drum_Fill|Drum Fill]] (or "fill-in") is a rhythmic embellishment or departure from the main groove that serves a specific musical purpose. Far from being random, professional fills are calculated tools used to bridge song sections, signal dynamic shifts, and guide the rest of the ensemble.

## Functional Categories

- **Transition Fills**: Used to connect two different sections of a song (e.g., from a **Verse** to a **Chorus**). These fills often increase in energy and volume to signify the upcoming shift.
- **Set-Up Fills**: Specifically designed to "set up" an upcoming **Ensemble Figure** (band hit). A [[Set-up_Fill|set-up fill]] of just one or two beats ensures the entire band hits the rhythm together with perfect precision.
- **Foreshadowing Fills**: A more advanced technique where a drummer plays a fill in the *subdivision* of the upcoming section (e.g., playing a triplet-based fill during the last bar of a straight-eighth section to signal a move to a **[[Shuffle|Shuffle]]**).
- **Embellishment Fills**: Small, subtle additions (like **[[Ghost_Notes|Ghost Notes]]**) within a groove that add "flavor" and "grease" without disrupting the time feel.

## Orchestration and Construction

Fills can be built using various rhythmic and technical elements:
- **Movement Across the Kit**: Typically starting on the [[Snare_Drum|snare drum]] and moving through the high, medium, and floor toms, often ending on a **Crash Cymbal** and **[[Bass_Drum|Bass Drum]]** on the downbeat of the next section.
- **Rudimental Logic**: Many world-class fills are constructed from basic **Rudiments**, such as single-stroke rolls, double-stroke rolls, **Ruffs**, or the **Hairta**.
- **The "One Stick" Technique**: For dramatic stops, a drummer might play a fill that ends with a single-stick [[Rimshot|rimshot]] while the free hand grabs/mutes a crash cymbal for total silence.

## Navigation and Reading

- **Written-Out Fills**: If a composer wants a specific melodic idea played, they will write out the exact notes of the fill. Failure to play a written-out fill as notated is a professional oversight.
- **Interpreted Fills**: Most charts simply show a blank [[Measure|measure]] with the text "Fill" or "ad-lib." Here, the drummer's role is to choose a fill that is "stylistically correct" and musically appropriate.
- **Phrase Awareness**: Fills almost always occur during the last [[Measure|measure]] (bar 4, 8, 12, or 16) of a musical phrase. Understanding these **Phrase Lengths** is key to placing fills naturally.

## Professional Advice
- **Conviction and Musicality**: "Repeats are good," but fills should be executed with confidence. A weak or hesitant fill can disrupt the band's time feel.
- **Subtlety**: Common fills leading back to a section repeat should generally be kept on the "subtle side" compared to those leading into a massive new section like a Chorus.
