# Four-Way Coordination: Accented Snare and Bass Drum

This study focuses on establishing individual dynamic control and independence by pairing [[Snare_Drum]] [[Accents|accents]] with specific [[Bass_Drum]] patterns. The primary objective is to maintain a consistent [[RLRL_Sticking]] (alternating) on the snare while the bass drum follows or deviates from the accented notes.

### Core Concepts
- **Dynamic Permutation**: The exercises challenge the player to execute [[Accents|accents]] both in unison with the bass drum and independently of it. This develops the ability to "decouple" the limbs.
- **[[Subdivision|Subdivision]] Focus**: Patterns typically move through 8th-note and 16th-note variations.
- **[[Hi-Hat|Hi-Hat]] Integration**: Once the snare and bass drum relationship is stable, a [[Hi-Hat_Ostinato]] (typically 8th-note stepping) is added to complete the four-way coordination.

### Pro-Tips and Pitfalls
- **Mirror Work**: Use a mirror to monitor stick heights. [[Accents|Accents]] should have a clear height differential compared to unaccented notes ([[Ghost_Notes|ghost notes]]).
- **[[Four-Part_Counting|Four-Part Counting]]**: Verbally count "1 e & a" while playing to ensure the rhythmic placement of the bass drum does not shift the snare's timing.
- **Common Pitfall**: Avoid "flamming" between the accented snare and the bass drum. They should land exactly together for a unified vertical alignment.
