# Upbeat [[Ensemble_Figures|Ensemble Figures]]

Upbeat [[Ensemble_Figures|Ensemble Figures]] are rhythmic hits that occur on the "off-beats" or syncopated partials, most commonly the **"&" (and)** of the [[Beat|beat]].

### The Challenge of Upbeats
When upbeats are played, musical momentum naturally increases. Musicians have a tendency to "push" or rush the [[Tempo|tempo]]. To counteract this, the drummer must provide a **strong downbeat** immediately prior to the upbeat figure. This anchors the ensemble and provides a clear reference point.

### Common Set-up Methods
For an upbeat figure (e.g., the "&" of [[Beat|Beat]] 1):
1. **Target**: The downbeat of the same [[Beat|beat]] ([[Beat|Beat]] 1).
2. **The "Kick-and-Hit"**: A common professional approach is to play a strong [[Bass_Drum]] on the downbeat and the [[Crash_Cymbal]]/[[Snare_Drum]] on the "&".
3. **Follow-through**: If a figure occurs on the "&" of 1, the drummer must ensure the snare drum [[Back-Beat]] on [[Beat|Beat]] 2 is played accurately to restore the [[Groove|groove]].

### Notation Examples
- **The "&" of [[Beat|Beat]] 1**: Often notated as an eighth note tied to a quarter note.
- **The "&" of [[Beat|Beat]] 2**: Requires a strong downbeat on [[Beat|Beat]] 2 to "launch" the figure on the "&".

### Pro-Tips
- **Don't Rush the Gap**: The space between the downbeat set-up and the upbeat hit is where most timing errors occur. Practice with a [[Metronome|metronome]] to ensure the eighth-note "gap" is exactly half a [[Beat|beat]].
- **Physicality**: Use a larger physical motion for the downbeat set-up to help the band "see" the time.
