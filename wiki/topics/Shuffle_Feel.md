# [[Shuffle|Shuffle]] Feel

The [[Shuffle|shuffle]] feel is a fundamental rhythmic concept, particularly prevalent in [[Blues_Rhythm|blues]], [[Jazz_Rhythm|jazz]], and certain styles of [[Rock_Music]]. It gives music a characteristic "swing" or "lilt."

## Definition

A [[Shuffle|shuffle]] feel is a triplet-based [[Groove|groove]] typically written in 4/4 time. It is often described as a "broken triplet feel," meaning that the middle triplet [[Subdivision|subdivision]] is not played.

*   **Notation vs. Performance**: While often notated as straight [[Eighth_Notes|eighth notes]] in a [[Chart|chart]], the presence of a specific symbol (a straight eighth note equals a swung eighth note triplet, Ex 7.6) at the beginning of the [[Chart|chart]] indicates that every written eighth note should be performed as a "broken" eighth-note [[Triplets]].
*   **Broken Triplet**: In a standard eighth-note triplet (1-e-&-a), the [[Shuffle|shuffle]] feel emphasizes the first and third partials, effectively creating a long-short rhythmic pattern (e.g., quarter note followed by an eighth note in the space of a triplet).

## Common Genres

*   **[[Blues|Blues]] Songs**: The [[Shuffle|shuffle]] is a cornerstone of [[Blues|blues]] music, providing its signature driving, laid-back feel.
*   **Medium [[Tempo|Tempo]] [[Jazz|Jazz]] Pieces**: Many [[Jazz|jazz]] tunes, especially those with a [[Blues|blues]] influence, utilize a [[Shuffle|shuffle]] feel.
*   **[[Rock|Rock]] Music**: Certain [[Rock|rock]] subgenres, particularly those with a [[Blues-Rock|blues-rock]] or boogie-woogie influence, heavily feature the [[Shuffle|shuffle]].

## Pro-Tips for Drummers

*   **Internalize the Triplet**: Even though the middle triplet isn't played, it's crucial to feel the underlying [[Triplets]] [[Subdivision|subdivision]]. Practice counting "1-and-a-2-and-a" to internalize it.
*   **Listen to Masters**: Listen extensively to drummers renowned for their [[Shuffle|shuffle]] feel (e.g., Bernard Purdie, Jeff Porcaro, Steve Gadd, various [[Blues|blues]] drummers). Pay attention to their [[Ghost_Notes|ghost notes]], [[Dynamics|dynamics]], and overall pocket.
*   **Dynamic Nuance**: The [[Shuffle|shuffle]] often benefits from subtle dynamic [[Accents|accents]]. Typically, the first part of the "long-short" pattern is slightly emphasized, giving it a push.
*   **Hi-Hat/Ride Pattern**: The classic [[Shuffle|shuffle]] pattern on the [[Hi-Hat]] or [[Ride_Cymbal]] is often a "chick-chick-a" or "ding-ding-a" sound, emphasizing the long-short feel.
*   **Practice with a [[Metronome|Metronome]]**: Start slowly with a [[Metronome_Practice|metronome]], focusing on the evenness of the long-short feel. Gradually increase the [[Tempo|tempo]].
