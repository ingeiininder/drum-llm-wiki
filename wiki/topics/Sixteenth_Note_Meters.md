# Sixteenth Note-Based Meters

Sixteenth note-based meters, such as **5/16** and **7/16**, apply the same principles as eighth-note meters but use the sixteenth note as the primary beat unit. These are common in contemporary fusion, progressive rock, and modern orchestral percussion.

### Counting and Subdivision
In a standard 4/4 bar, sixteenth notes are grouped in fours (1-e-&-a). In 5/16, the performer counts five individual sixteenth notes (1-2-3-4-5). 

*   **Rate of Travel**: All sixteenth notes must travel at the same rate during the transition from [[4/4 Time]] to [[5/16 Time]].
*   **Fluctuating Beat Values**: Advanced exercises often cycle through 4/4, 5/8, and 5/16. 
    *   **4/4**: 4 beats (Quarter note unit)
    *   **5/8**: 5 beats (Eighth note unit)
    *   **5/16**: 5 beats (Sixteenth note unit)

### Technical Execution
When playing [[Groove]] exercises in these meters, the [[Bass Drum]] and [[Snare Drum]] patterns often become highly syncopated. 

**Metronome Setup**: For exercises involving 16th-note meters, set the metronome to sixteenth notes (e.g., 160 BPM). This ensures that the "micro-timing" remains consistent even as the "macro-meter" changes.

> [!CAUTION] **Common Pitfall**
> A common mistake is to "swing" or "shuffle" the sixteenth notes in 5/16. They must remain strictly "straight" to maintain the mathematical relationship with the preceding 4/4 or 5/8 measures.
