Chapter 2: Performance Notes and Sound Letter A Letter A (the verse) features a 16th-note based 6/8 groove, which is preformed as a four-bar phrase: All four bars are played on a medium-light hi-hat and utilize a cross-stick-articulation on the snare drum alongside an "off-the-head" bass drum sound: The four-bar phrase looks like this: Ex 3.6
Tight Hi-hat/Cross-stick Snare
This 6/8-ballad groove has been played on thousands of popular recordings Although it is technically simple to play, it is quite difficult to make this groove feel and sound good. You must pay particular attention to make sure that each limb lines up with one another Thus; each time you play a snare (or bass drum) that lands with the hi-hat; they should be in unison, and no flams should be present Please focus on the swung 16th-note (actually I6th-triplet) bass drum on the"&"of 6 (and "&"of 3) as well Ifthe time straightens out to groups of two (i.e. straight I6th notes), you will lose the proper feel:
Cross-Stick Sound In order to get a strong; full and warm cross-stick sound, turn the stick around and use the butt end The bead (tip) of your stick should be placed just "off-center" between the eight and nine oclock position on the head, and the butt end will be off the drum between the two and three oclock position like this: Ex 3.7 (left):
In order to make the stroke, grip the stick in your normal fashion. Then "throw"it into the drum and take your four fingers off the stick At this point your fingers should flatten and you will keep the stick on the head (and rim) by applying pressure: To make the next cross-stick stroke, grab the stick with your fingers again and repeat (above) Ex 3.8
