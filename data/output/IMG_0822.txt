Unit three reading | techNIque perfOrmance Chapter 1: Charley Wilcoxon Solo No.1 One of the most effective ways to thoroughly learn something is through variety: Exploring various applications; perspectives and combinations will enable you to build a stronger sense of what this new information can be capable of. Rudimental snare drum solos offer opportunities to further enhance and grow our understanding of the snare drum rudiments we've learned thus far
For these reasons, we will be exploring many of the rudimental snare drum solos from Charley Wilcoxons iconic book, The All-American Drummer 150 Rudimental Solos. These solos will reveal how the ability to combine various rudiments is not only essential to a drummer's vocabulary, but is also an extremely useful musical tool. Try to look at each solo as a piece of music not an exercise. Paying attention to the dynamic markings will allow these solos to have color and life Strive for musical expression don't just play a combination of rudiments: that is not art Use these solos as opportunities to stretch your musical capabilities and reap the rewards of what honest expression offers
For this unit we will be exploring Solo No.1. This 16-bar solo, in the time signature of 2/4,incorporates a handful of rudiments we've studied individually: Here, we get to combine these rudiments to create a piece of music. The rudimental breakdown is as follows:
Bar 1: Flam Taps (as eighth notes) Bar 2: Flam Paradiddles Bar 5: Flamacue with a left-hand Flam Tap ' (as I6th notes) on the"&" of beat 2 Bar 6: Flam Taps' (as eighth notes) Bar 7: Flam Paradiddles Bar 8: Alternating Flams Bar 10: Single Paradiddles Bars 11 & 12: Triple Paradiddles Bar 14: Single Paradiddles Bar 16: Alternating Flams (ending on beat 2)
As with learning any new idea or concept on the drums, the first priority is developing the coordination (assuming the reading is not an issue): Once the mechanics have been worked out; then and only then do you begin to address the dynamic markings Dynamics require a heightened sense of control, which makes learning something new too much for the brain/body to handle all at once: Develop the coordination first, then slowly apply the dynamics This will greatly speed up the learning process, and the outcome will be of higher quality as well:
