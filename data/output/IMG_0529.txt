Chapter 4: Bass Drum Technique Heel Up & Heel Down Bass drum technique is one of the most widely overlooked aspects in effectively playing and studying the drums Just as we explore and develop various stick techniques to gain the freedom that allow us to express ourselves, we should also have a fundamental understanding of how to control our feet.
Ex 3.16
Basic bass drum technique generally falls into two categories: "heel down" and "heel up" The heel-down position is just what the name implies; drop your heel to the ground (or base ofthe pedal plate) and then allow your foot to rest on the remaining surface of the pedal (Ex 3.16). This position solely relies on the ankle to be the pivot point in transferring the energy from the foot to the pedal:
The advantages of this technique that it allows you to play very soft and controlled,and it will help maintain your overall balance (even though we learned in the previous chapter our balance should be centered on out seat) The disadvantage of heel-down technique is it isolates the shin muscle   (tibialis anterior). Whenever you press down on the bass drum pedal this muscle is doing all the work, and it can quickly become fatigued ifyoure playing fast and/or loud passages of music
Ex 3.17
Heel-up position is somewhat the same as heel-down; but the heel is slightly raised off of the pedal board about one to two inches (Ex: 3.17) . Heel-up offers more options in terms of dynamics and speed The heel-up technique is also less fatiguing on the shin muscle: Since we have raised our heel, the shin is no longer isolated; others muscles in our leg can now take some of the responsibility: This position allows us to use some of our upper leg for energy and power; however weare not relying on our_upper leg_to_provide_theenergy for thebass drum stroke The focus is still on the pivoting of the ankle to provide the stroke; just as we did with the heel-down position:
Think of heel-up as a "heel-down motion with a heel-up position" The pivoting of the ankle should be identical in both positions A good way to gauge if you're using too much upper leg in heel-up position is to watch your knee as youre playing: Ifyour knee is "bobbing"up and down like a piston, youre using too much upper leg: Of course this will change when you have to play accents or loud sections of a song, but generally the knee should only be moving sympathetically within the stroke:
